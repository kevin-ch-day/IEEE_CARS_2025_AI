\section{Discussion}

The results should be interpreted as evidence from a controlled defensive study, not as proof that real-world malware was authored by AI. This distinction is important because AI authorship is difficult to establish from compiled artifacts alone. A defensible approach is to compare known experimental conditions and report observable differences.

The study also has ethical and safety implications. AI-assisted cybersecurity research should be performed in isolated environments, with clear documentation, and with a defensive purpose. The goal of this work is to improve understanding of detection, analysis, and defensive readiness rather than to enable misuse.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The results may depend on the selected artifacts, the AI tools used, the prompts or workflow, the analysis environment, and the detection products available during testing. Future work should expand the dataset, improve behavioral instrumentation, and test whether findings generalize across different malware categories, platforms, or security tools.
