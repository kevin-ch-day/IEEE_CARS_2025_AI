\section{Background and Related Work}

Large language models are increasingly used for programming assistance, explanation, and cybersecurity learning. Prior work has examined both the benefits and risks of these systems, including their use in secure coding, vulnerability discovery, malware analysis, and adversarial automation.

Malware research traditionally relies on static analysis, dynamic analysis, behavioral observation, and detection-engine results. Static analysis examines code, metadata, imports, permissions, strings, and structural artifacts. Dynamic analysis observes runtime behavior such as process activity, file changes, registry or system modifications, network traffic, and command-and-control patterns.

AI-assisted malware research creates a measurement challenge. Compiled artifacts rarely preserve direct evidence showing whether an AI system contributed to development. Therefore, this study avoids unsupported claims of AI authorship and instead evaluates whether controlled AI-assisted workflows produce measurable differences that are relevant to defensive analysis.
